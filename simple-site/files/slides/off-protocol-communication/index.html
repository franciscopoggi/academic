Off-Protocol Communication
Francisco Poggi
University of Mannheim
SAET · Rio de Janeiro · 2026

Motivation

  • Communication is sometimes organized around some protocol: who speaks, when, what messages are allowed.
    • E.g., peer review, court procedure.
    • Organizations in general: internal reports, expert advice.
  • Designing these protocols can be a powerful tool to shape outcomes.
  • However, participants can sometimes talk off protocol, potentially undermining the mechanism.
Question
When does off-protocol communication undermine the mechanism, and how can we design protocols that are robust to off-protocol communication?

Roadmap

  • Application: Experts with career concerns.
    • Results: robust organizations must promote experts more often and/or produce less informed recommendations.
  • General model
  • General results
    • Revelation Principle;
    • Equivalence.
Part I
Application: Experts with Career Concerns

Experts with Career Concerns

  • Players: 2 senders ($i=1,2$), 1 receiver.
  • One sender is an expert (E), the other is a quack (Q).
  • State $\omega\in\{L,R\}$. Uniform prior.
  • Each sender observes a conditionally independent binary signal $s_i\in \{l,r\}$, with precisions $p_E$ and $p_Q$. For exposition: $p_Q=1/2$ (quack is completely uninformed).
  • The receiver must
    • choose an action $a\in \{L,R\}$;
    • choose a promotion $\pi \in \{1,2\}$.

Payoffs

  • Everyone receives $1$ if $a=\omega$.
  • Sender $i$ receives an additional bonus $B>0$ iff promoted ($\pi=i$).
  • Receiver receives a bonus $D>0$ if the expert is promoted ($\pi=E$).

Direct Mechanisms

A Direct Mechanism asks each sender to report:

  • whether they are the expert,
  • their signal realization,

and gives the receiver an action recommendation $(\hat{a},\hat{\pi})$.

We focus on a symmetric family of DM parametrized by:

$$x= \text{Pr}(\hat{a}=\omega\mid \text{truthful reports}) \qquad y= \text{Pr}(\hat{\pi}=E\mid\text{truthful reports})$$

When reports are inconsistent (either both senders claim to be the expert or none), the mechanism draws $(\hat{a},\hat{\pi})$ uniformly at random.

Feasibility, Expert Truthfulness, and Obedience

  • Feasibility: $x\leq p_E$. The recommendation cannot be more precise than the expert's signal.
  • Obedience: the receiver follows recommendations if $x\geq 1/2$, $y\geq 1/2$.
  • Truthfulness — Expert. Given that the quack reports truthfully, $$x+yB \;\geq\; \max\left\{\frac{1}{2}+\frac{B}{2}\,,\; (1-x)+yB\right\}$$
    • Slack under obedience.

Quack Truthfulness

Truthfulness — Quack. Given that the expert reports truthfully,

$$x+(1-y)B \;\geq\; \frac{1}{2} + \frac{B}{2}$$

Cases where it is satisfied, given $x\geq 1/2$:

  • $B=0$: no rents from promotion;
  • $y=1/2$: promotion is independent of expertise.
Takeaway
Quack lies when $B$ is large and $y$ is close to $1$.

IC Direct Mechanisms

Off-Protocol Communication

Timing.

Reports $(e,\hat{s})$  $\rightarrow$  Recommendation $(\hat{a},\hat{\pi})$  $\rightarrow$  Confession  $\rightarrow$  Actions $(a,\pi)$

Confession stage:

  • Senders can send a cheap-talk message to the receiver.

Expert Might Sabotage the Mechanism

Fix a Direct Mechanism with $y<1$.

Consider the following double deviation by the expert:

  • Sabotage: swap the report signal ($l \leftrightarrow r$).
  • Confess to the receiver:
Confession
"I am the expert, and I lied in my report. Flip the recommended action. A quack would never benefit from asking you to flip, so you can trust me and promote me."

Is this confession credible? Apply a concept close to Cho & Kreps (1987) intuitive criterion and Farrell (1993) neologism-proofness.

Does It Work?

If the receiver believes the confession, the expert's payoff is

$$x + B \qquad \text{instead of} \qquad x + y B$$
  • It's always profitable (since $y<1$). The claim a quack would not benefit is what remains to verify.
  • If the receiver were to believe the confession, the quack maximizes his payoff by claiming to be the expert in the mechanism and lying in the confession. Beneficial iff:
$$\frac{1}{2}+B \;\geq\; x +(1-y)B$$

Mechanisms Robust to Off-Protocol Communication

Intuition

Consider the mechanism with maximal action accuracy ($x=p_E$) and lowest promotion of the expert ($y=1/2$).

  • This may be optimal for some designer's objectives.
  • IC is slack: quack gets promoted half of the time, so no gains from mimicking the expert.
  • But this mechanism is not robust to off-protocol communication: the expert's confession is maximally credible.
  • So to obtain a mechanism that survives off-protocol communication, we need some distortion, either:
    • raise $y$: make promotion informative, so that the quack is tempted to sabotage — this kills credibility;
    • lower $x$: reduce the accuracy cost the quack pays for sabotaging, again killing credibility.
Part II
General Model

General Model

  • $n+1$ players: Receiver ($i=0$) and $n$ Senders ($i=1,\dots,n$).
  • Senders have private types $\theta_i\in \Theta_i$
    • $\theta=(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_n)$ drawn from $\mu\in \Delta(\Theta)$.
  • Receiver chooses action $a\in A$.
  • For every player, final payoff given by $u_i:A\times \Theta \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$.
  • Timing:
    • Senders observe their private information;
    • Communication: protocol + off-protocol;
    • Receiver takes action;
    • Payoffs are realized.

Protocols

Communication Protocol
$(\{M_i\}_{i=1}^n,\tau)$ consisting of a set of messages $M_i$ for each sender and a function $\tau:M_1\times\dots\times M_n \rightarrow \Delta(M_0)$.

Interpretation: senders simultaneously submit a message $m_i\in M_i$ to a black box that produces a message for the receiver.

Strategies: $\sigma_i: \Theta_i \rightarrow \Delta(M_i)$ and $\sigma_0:M_0 \rightarrow \Delta(A)$.

Equilibrium:

  • every action in the support is a best-response given the player's information.
Part III
General Results

Revelation Principle

Direct revelation protocol
Communication protocol where $M_i=\Theta_i$ for all $i$ and $M_0=A$. We use $\Gamma: \Theta \rightarrow \Delta(A)$ to denote DRPs.

Following Myerson (1982) we define obedience and truthfulness:

  • DRP is obedient if the receiver finds it optimal to follow the recommended action, assuming truthful reporting;
  • DRP is truthful if reporting truthfully is a BNE, assuming obedience.

We call a DRP IC if it is truthful and obedient.

Revelation Principle — Myerson (1982)
WLOG to focus on truthful and obedient DRPs.

Confessions

Fix a protocol $(\{M_i\},\tau)$. Let $P_i:= \Theta_i \times M_i$.

Confession
A confession for sender $i$ is a tuple $(T,\hat{\sigma}_0)$, where $T\subseteq P_i$ and $\hat{\sigma}_0:M_0\rightarrow \Delta(A)$ is a suggested action rule.

Fix an equilibrium $\sigma$; a confession by sender $i$ is credible iff:

  • switching $\sigma_0 \rightarrow \hat{\sigma}_0$ is only profitable for type-message combinations of $i$ in $T$;
  • $\hat{\sigma}_0$ is a best-response of the receiver, conditional on every element of $T$.
Confession-proofness
A protocol–equilibrium pair is confession-proof if there's no credible confession.

A Confession-Proof Revelation Principle

Revelation Principle
For every protocol and equilibrium that is confession-proof, there is an IC Direct Revelation Protocol that is confession-proof and has the same outcomes.

Single Sender Result

Suppose $n=1$.

An IC DRP is neologism-proof if there is no type set $T\subseteq \Theta$ and action $\alpha\in \Delta(A)$ such that:

  • $\alpha$ is a best-response for the receiver conditional on $T$;
  • $\alpha$ is preferred to $\Gamma(\theta)$ by sender $\theta$ if and only if $\theta\in T$.
Proposition
For $n=1$, the set of neologism-proof DRPs coincides with the set of confession-proof DRPs.
  • For $n>1$ the equivalence does not hold!
  • Intuition: for $n=1$, the sender can pin down, given the protocol, what distribution of messages the receiver observes.

Conclusion

  • When designing communication protocols, it's important to account for off-protocol communication.
    • A minimal robustness notion is accounting for incentives to confess deviations.
  • We study the problem of designing communication protocols to elicit experts' information when:
    • experts have career concerns;
    • experts can communicate outside the protocol.
  • We find that:
    • when career concerns are large, protocols that produce very informed recommendations cannot promote the expert too often, to avoid quack misreporting;
    • when career concerns are small, a protocol that produces very informed recommendations must promote the expert sufficiently often.
